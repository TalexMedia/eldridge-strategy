02 The Equity You've Built

Three Strengths Already In Place

All three are earned, all three are working. Any new video would build on what's already here.

Scale equity. Eldridge runs more than $70 billion in assets under management across Capital Management (diversified credit, GP solutions, real estate credit, sports & entertainment) and Wealth Solutions (Security Benefit and Everly Life). The firm operates from fourteen office cities across the Americas, Europe, and Asia, and the holding-company architecture lets it move across asset classes most allocators have to pick between. The structural advantage is real, and it lands harder when a viewer can see it told by the people inside it.

Demand equity. The chairman is on Bloomberg, the CEO is on Bloomberg, the Co-Presidents and Heads are on Milken panels, and the Capital Allocators podcast was willing to frame Boehly's episode as "The Next Berkshire Hathaway at Eldridge." There is an audience that wants to watch Eldridge talk about the markets, the businesses, and the thesis. Most B2B firms work for years to build that demand. Eldridge already has it.

Story equity. The impact page and the news page together hold most of the raw material a documentary shop would want: a $4.3 billion real estate close in March, a Swiss market entry in April, a recycled-cotton fiber business processing 84,300 metric tons a year, a film about Sing Sing reaching nearly a million viewers behind bars, and a Dodgers Foundation backing more than 11.5 million young people. Each is a specific, datable story, and none has yet been told as a film with Eldridge as the producer.

What Eldridge's public presence does not yet contain is documentary storytelling that Eldridge owns and distributes. The principles on the homepage, the transactions on the news page, and the operating businesses on the impact page are the raw material. What follows is a short proposal on what that material looks like as long-form video.

03 Lead With Value

The Legendeering Framework, In One Page

The methodology behind every Talex production. Five directives, one core principle.

Legendeering is Talex's proprietary strategy. The core principle is straightforward: lead with value, always. The brand earns trust by giving without expectation, and that trust grows over time because people keep choosing to watch.

Five directives drive it:

  • ·Give Without Expectation. Value delivered without strings. The kind of generosity that builds loyalty money cannot buy.
  • ·Be Relentlessly Authentic. Say true things. Show real work. Speak like a human being.
  • ·It's Not About You. It's About Them. The audience has to come first. It's all about their wants and needs and making something they will choose to watch.
  • ·Start With Story, Follow With Facts. Story gives information shape, context, and emotional weight. Facts alone rarely move people.
  • ·Consistency Is Key. Trust is built in moments, repeated over time.

Eldridge is the expert in allocating capital across credit, real estate, sports, and insurance. Talex would add the long-form video: documentary films built on the deals Eldridge already closes and the operating businesses Eldridge already backs. 04 Three Concepts For Eldridge

Three Long-Form Concepts For The Eldridge Brand

Each one builds on equity Eldridge has already earned. Each one is something only Eldridge could make.

01
"Inside The Portfolio"
A returning documentary series in which each episode follows one Eldridge-backed operating business through a defining moment. The portfolio company is the on-camera protagonist, whether that is the leadership of Recover walking a camera through the recycling line that processes 84,300 metric tons of cotton a year, the One Beverly Hills team explaining the geothermal and water systems behind a $4.3 billion development, or the Montana Renewables crew converting waste feedstock into low-emission fuel. The Eldridge Partner whose strategy backed the deal appears as the strategic guide who walks the viewer through what was actually at stake and how the capital got committed. The audience is institutional LPs, family offices, and peer firms, the same people who already watch the Bloomberg and Milken clips. Each film doubles. Eldridge gets a portfolio piece that proves the thesis through someone else's story. The portfolio company gets the definitive film of the most consequential chapter in its history, the kind of film that lives on its own channels long after the financing is closed.
02
"The Mechanics Of A Close"
A returning short series that walks the audience through one Eldridge transaction at a time, from inside the room. How the deal was sourced, how it got structured, how it was priced, how it closed. The Eldridge Partner who led the deal is the on-camera narrator; the portfolio company, borrower, or counterparty is the second voice in the conversation. First episode candidates write themselves from the recent news page: the One Beverly Hills $4.3 billion close with John Cole, the Conduit Power $200 million equipment facility, the LIKA Swiss entry with three coordinated development loans, the Stonebriar leadership story that Monitor Daily already profiled. The audience is institutional LPs, family offices, allocators, and peer firms, which is the same audience that already chose to listen to the Capital Allocators episode on Eldridge. The series answers the question they always have when they see an announcement on a Bloomberg ticker: how does a deal like that actually get done.
03
"The Bench Sessions"
A teaching-format short series. Each Eldridge Partner or Head takes one concept they have spent a career on and puts it on documentary film. John Cole on what real estate credit looks like at the scale of a $4.3 billion close. Jeff Forlizzi on diversified credit through a fragmented cycle. Jeff Wilbur on the economics of sports investment. Nicholas Sandler on the rebuilt institutional asset-based finance market he was just on stage about at Milken. Douglas Wolff on how the insurance side of a holding-company model actually works. Each session is shot once to a documentary portrait standard, lives on Eldridge's site and channel, and stands on its own as the kind of fifteen-minute piece an allocator would set aside time to watch before a meeting. The series doubles as proof of bench depth in one scrollable place.
